## 1. Federal Conformity/Decoupling Master Table

California conforms to the IRC as of a specific date (rolling conformity with selective decoupling). Always check FTB Publication 1001 for the applicable tax year. Below are the most critical areas — flag these every time.

### Areas Where California DECOUPLES (Does NOT Conform)

| Federal Provision | California Treatment | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **§199A QBI Deduction** | Not allowed. California taxes 100% of pass-through income with no QBI deduction. | Major hit for S-corp/partnership owners. Can mean 9.3–13.3% CA tax on income that gets 20% federal deduction. §199A is now **permanent** (OBBBA) — CA's continued decoupling is an even bigger planning point. PTE election (Section 3) is the primary workaround. |
| **Bonus Depreciation (§168(k))** | Historically decoupled. California has not allowed 100% bonus depreciation. Must use regular MACRS or §179 (with CA-specific limits). | Creates book-tax difference and separate depreciation schedules for CA. |
| **§179 Expensing** | California has its own lower limit ($25,000 deduction / $200,000 phase-out — verify current year). Drastically lower than federal. | Must maintain separate fixed-asset depreciation for CA returns. |
| **Opportunity Zones (§1400Z-2)** | Full decoupling. California does not recognize OZ deferral, basis step-up, or exclusion. | OZ investments provide zero California benefit. |
| **SALT Deduction Cap** | N/A at state level — California never had its own SALT cap. Federal SALT cap now $40,000 (OBBBA, 2025–2029; $20K MFS), with AGI phasedown at $500K+ (30% reduction rate, floor $10K). This significantly changes PTE election math — at $40K cap, fewer taxpayers need the PTE workaround. See Section 3 for updated break-even analysis. | PTE tax election (AB 150) remains viable for high-SALT taxpayers and those above the AGI phasedown. |
| **Net Operating Losses** | California has its own NOL rules. Periodic suspension of NOL usage (happened in 2020, 2024 — check current status). California limits NOL deduction to $30M in some years. Different carryforward periods historically. | Must track CA NOL separately. Suspension years create cash flow planning issues. |
| **Excess Business Loss Limitation** | California conforms to §461(l) but verify annual status. | Threshold amounts differ — check FTB instructions. |
| **Charitable Contribution Limits** | California generally conforms to federal percentages but has historically had differences in carryover periods and specific provisions. | Usually not a major issue but verify for large gifts. |
| **TCJA Individual Rate Changes** | California has its own progressive brackets (1%–12.3% + 1% Mental Health Tax over $1M). Federal rate changes do not affect CA rates. | California's top marginal rate of 13.3% is the highest state income tax rate in the nation. |
| **Federal Standard Deduction** | California has its own standard deduction (~$5,540 single / $11,080 MFJ — verify current year). Much lower than federal ($16,100/$32,200 for 2026, permanent per OBBBA). | Most CA taxpayers who take the federal standard deduction may still itemize on CA. New OBBBA senior deduction ($6,000/person, 2025–2028) further widens the gap. |
| **Federal Estate/Gift Tax Exemption** | California has no state estate tax or gift tax. | Only federal estate/gift taxes apply. CA inheritance is income-tax-free (but IRD items still taxed). Federal exemption now $15M/person permanent (OBBBA, indexed). Far fewer CA clients will face federal estate tax. |

### Areas Where California CONFORMS (With Caveats)

| Federal Provision | California Treatment | Caveats |
|---|---|---|
| **§1031 Like-Kind Exchanges** | Generally conforms | Clawback rule: if you do a §1031 exchange and the replacement property is out of CA, the FTB can claw back the deferred gain when you sell or if you leave CA. Report on FTB 3840. |
| **Installment Sales (§453)** | Generally conforms | Watch for residency changes during installment period — sourcing of gain to CA. |
| **Passive Activity Rules (§469)** | Generally conforms | CA applies same rules but separate passive activity computations may differ due to decoupling items affecting CA income. |
| **§121 Home Sale Exclusion** | Conforms | Same $250K/$500K exclusion applies for CA. |
| **Retirement Plans** | Generally conforms | CA taxes traditional IRA/401(k) distributions. Roth qualified distributions are CA-tax-free. |
| **Capital Gains** | CA taxes capital gains as ordinary income — no preferential rate | This is a massive planning consideration. Federal 0%/15%/20% + 3.8% NIIT vs. CA up to 13.3%. |

### Critical Note: Capital Gains Treatment
California taxes ALL capital gains as ordinary income. There is no preferential capital gains rate. Combined with the 13.3% top rate, this makes CA the most expensive state for realizing capital gains. This drives enormous planning activity:
- Timing of gain recognition
- Installment sale strategies to spread income across years/brackets
- §1031 exchanges (but watch the clawback)
- Charitable remainder trusts (spread gain over trust term)
- Residency change planning (but beware FTB aggressive positions — see Section 4)

## 2. California Income Tax Rates & Structure

### Personal Income Tax Brackets (2024 — verify for current year)
- 1% on first $10,412 (single) / $20,824 (MFJ)
- 2% on $10,413–$24,684 / $20,825–$49,368
- 4% on $24,685–$38,959 / $49,369–$77,918
- 6% on $38,960–$54,081 / $77,919–$108,162
- 8% on $54,082–$68,350 / $108,163–$136,700
- 9.3% on $68,351–$349,137 / $136,701–$698,274
- 10.3% on $349,138–$418,961 / $698,275–$837,922
- 11.3% on $418,962–$698,271 / $837,923–$1,396,542
- 12.3% on $698,272+ / $1,396,543+
- **Mental Health Services Tax (Prop 63)**: Additional 1% on taxable income over $1,000,000 — bringing the effective top rate to 13.3%

### Corporate Tax
- 8.84% on net income (C-corps)
- 1.5% on net income (S-corps — minimum franchise tax of $800)
- Minimum franchise tax: $800 (waived for first year under certain conditions)
- LLC fee: graduated based on total income ($0–$6,000), plus $800 annual tax
- LLC classified as partnership: $800 minimum tax + LLC fee based on total income

### Alternative Minimum Tax
- California has its own AMT (7% rate)
- Different preference items than federal (ISO exercise is a preference for both)
- California AMT is a particular trap for tech employees exercising large ISO grants

## 3. PTE Tax Election (AB 150)

The California Pass-Through Entity (PTE) tax election is the primary SALT cap workaround. Know this cold.

### Mechanics
- Elective tax on qualified net income of consenting qualified PTE owners
- Tax rate: 9.3% on the PTE's qualified net income
- PTE makes the election and pays the tax
- Each consenting owner receives a nonrefundable credit against their personal CA income tax (with limited carryforward)
- Net effect: converts nondeductible state tax (blocked by SALT cap) into a deductible business expense at the entity level

### Eligible Entities
- S corporations, partnerships, LLCs taxed as partnerships
- NOT available for single-member LLCs (disregarded entities) or sole proprietorships

### Qualified Taxpayers
- Individuals, trusts, estates that are partners/shareholders/members
- Must consent to the election
- C corporation partners/shareholders are NOT qualified taxpayers (but entity can still elect if it has at least one qualifying owner)

### Key Limitations & Traps
- Guaranteed payments to partners are EXCLUDED from qualified net income
- The credit is nonrefundable — if CA tax liability is less than the credit, excess carries forward (5 years)
- Must be elected on an original, timely-filed return (no late elections)
- Payment deadlines: first installment by June 15, remainder by original return due date
- If a partner is in a lower CA bracket, the 9.3% PTE tax may exceed what they'd owe — need to model per-partner to ensure net benefit
- Interaction with other credits: PTE credit applied after other credits, reducing benefit for taxpayers with significant other credits

### Planning Considerations (Updated for OBBBA $40K SALT Cap)
- **OBBBA impact on PTE election**: The $40K SALT cap (2025–2029) reduces the universe of taxpayers who benefit from PTE election. Key scenarios:
  - **SALT < $40K and MAGI < $500K**: PTE election likely provides **no net benefit** — taxpayer can deduct full SALT on Schedule A. Election still offsets CA's §199A decoupling but the SALT deduction alone may be sufficient.
  - **SALT > $40K**: PTE election still valuable — entity-level deduction bypasses the cap entirely.
  - **MAGI > $500K**: SALT cap phases down toward $10K (30% reduction rate). PTE election regains significant value as effective cap shrinks.
  - **MAGI > ~$600K+**: Effective SALT cap at floor ($10K) — PTE election math is essentially the same as pre-OBBBA.
- Model the net benefit for each owner individually — not all owners benefit equally
- Compare: owner pays CA tax at marginal rate (nondeductible above SALT cap) vs. entity pays 9.3% PTE tax (federally deductible)
- For owners in the 12.3%+ bracket with high MAGI, the PTE election almost always makes sense
- For owners in lower brackets or with MAGI < $500K and SALT < $40K, the math may not work
- **Note**: SALT cap reverts to $10K in 2030 — PTE election value will snap back for all taxpayers. Plan for sunset.
- Multi-state owners: check if other states give credit for CA PTE tax paid

## 4. Residency Rules & Multi-State Taxation

### California Residency Determination

The FTB uses a facts-and-circumstances test for residency. Key factors:

**Domicile** — Your true, fixed, permanent home. Intent to remain or return is key.

**Statutory Residency** — Present in California for other than a temporary or transitory purpose (no specific day count, but >9 months creates a strong presumption).

**Safe Harbor for Nonresidents** — An individual domiciled in CA is treated as a nonresident if:
- Present in CA for ≤45 days during the tax year
- Has a filing requirement in another state
- Has >$250,000 in CA-source income (FTB uses this threshold to scrutinize)

### Residency Factors the FTB Examines
- Physical location of your home(s)
- Where you spend the most time
- Family location (spouse, dependents)
- Driver's license state
- Voter registration
- Professional licenses (including CPA!)
- Bank accounts, safe deposit boxes
- Social ties, club memberships, religious affiliations
- Business connections
- Location of personal property (vehicles, art, jewelry)
- Mailing address used for tax returns, financial accounts

### Part-Year Residency
- Income allocated between resident and nonresident periods
- CA-source income is taxable in both periods
- Non-CA-source income only taxable during the resident period
- Use Schedule CA to adjust income items

### California-Source Income (Nonresidents & Part-Year)
- Wages: sourced where services are performed (duty days allocation)
- Business income: apportioned using single-sales-factor for most businesses, or specific allocation for services
- Real property income: sourced to CA if property is in CA
- Gain on sale of partnership interest: sourced based on partnership's CA apportionment factors (FTB aggressive position vs. Metropoulos)
- Stock options/RSUs: California sources based on allocation of service period (grant-to-vest) in CA
- Retirement/pension: generally not CA-source for nonresidents (4 USC §114)
- Intangible income: sourced to state of residence (generally) but FTB has asserted taxing authority on certain intangible transactions with CA nexus

### FTB Aggressive Positions on Departing Residents
The FTB is notoriously aggressive with tech founders and high-income individuals who move out of California:
- Stock option / RSU vesting: CA claims right to tax the CA-service-period allocation even after departure
- Deferred compensation: CA claims right to tax based on allocation of services performed in CA during earning period
- Sale of business: FTB may argue goodwill has CA-source component
- Installment sales: gain sourced back to CA for the portion attributable to CA activities
- **The "departure audit"**: FTB routinely audits high-income individuals who change residency, questioning whether the move was genuine
- Key defense: document everything — lease termination, home sale, utility disconnects, new state license/registration, school enrollment, employment contracts

### Multi-State Apportionment
- Single-sales-factor apportionment (post-Prop 39) for most businesses
- Market-based sourcing for services and intangibles (RTC §25136)
- Water's-edge election vs. worldwide combined reporting: election available for multinational groups, annual election with 7-year binding period
- Throwback rule: California does not have a throwback rule (eliminated for tax years beginning on or after 2013)

## 5. California-Specific Deductions & Credits

### Key California Credits
- **California R&D credit (RTC §17052.12 / §23609)**: Similar to but differs from IRC §41. 24% of qualified research expenses over base amount (15% for certain small businesses). No expiration — unlimited carryforward. Often more valuable than federal credit for CA-heavy research operations.
- **California Competes Tax Credit**: Negotiated credit for businesses that want to relocate to or stay in CA. Application-based through GO-Biz.
- **Low-Income Housing Credit**: California's version, separate from federal LIHTC.
- **New Employment Credit**: For businesses in designated geographic areas.
- **College Access Tax Credit**: 50% credit for contributions to Cal Grant program.

### California-Specific Deductions
- California standard deduction is much lower than federal
- California itemized deductions: generally follow federal but remember SALT is not capped at state level (you deduct full state taxes paid on the CA return... wait, no — you do NOT deduct CA income taxes on your CA return, but you can deduct other state income taxes paid)
- Mortgage interest: California generally conforms to federal limits

## 6. FTB Audit & Enforcement Patterns

### Statute of Limitations
- 4 years from original due date or date filed, whichever is later (RTC §19057) — NOTE: one year longer than federal
- 8 years if >25% gross income understatement
- Unlimited if fraud or no return
- Federal change: 2 years from date of federal RAR to file amended CA return or FTB assessment (RTC §18622)

### FTB Protest & Appeals
- 60-day protest period after Notice of Proposed Assessment (NPA)
- Protest to FTB, then appeal to Office of Tax Appeals (OTA) — replaced BOE for income tax
- OTA decisions are precedential if designated
- Superior Court appeal available after OTA

### Common FTB Audit Targets
- Residency changes (especially departing high-income)
- Stock option / RSU sourcing
- R&D credit claims
- Pass-through entity allocations
- Independent contractor vs. employee classification (AB 5 / Prop 22 intersection)
- Cannabis businesses (still §280E issues at federal level, CA has some differences)
- Cryptocurrency/digital asset reporting

## 7. California Property Tax (Prop 13 & Related)

### Prop 13 Basics (1978)
- Assessed value: base year value (1975–76 or purchase/construction) + max 2%/year inflation adjustment
- Reassessment triggers: change in ownership, new construction
- Tax rate: 1% of assessed value (plus voter-approved bonded indebtedness, special assessments)

### Key Propositions
- **Prop 58/193** (now modified by Prop 19): parent-child / grandparent-grandchild exclusion
- **Prop 19 (2021)**: Dramatically limited parent-child exclusion — primary residence only, $1M cap on assessed value difference, must be used as child's primary residence within 1 year. Eliminated exclusion for all other property.
- **Prop 19 Portability**: Homeowners 55+ (or disabled, or disaster victims) can transfer base year value to a replacement home anywhere in CA, up to 3 times.
- **Change in ownership**: transfers via trusts, LLCs, partnerships — look-through rules (BOE Letter To Assessors)

### Planning Considerations
- Entity structuring to avoid reassessment (but BOE scrutinizes)
- Prop 19 planning for aging parents / family homes
- Supplemental tax bills: immediate reassessment difference for change-in-ownership year
- Property tax appeals: decline-in-value assessments during market downturns

## 8. Community Property Rules

California is a community property state. Tax implications:

- Community property income: each spouse reports 50% regardless of who earned it
- Separate property: income from separate property is separate (CA rule — differs from some CP states)
- Full basis step-up: on death of first spouse, BOTH halves of community property get stepped-up basis (§1014(b)(6)) — this is a major planning advantage vs. common-law states
- MFS in community property states: each spouse reports 50% of community income
- Divorce: property settlements under §1041 (nonrecognition), but watch for CA-specific community property tracing rules
- Domestic partnerships: same treatment as married spouses for CA tax purposes (and federal since Windsor/Obergefell)
